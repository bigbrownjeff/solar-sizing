The number the configurator won't show you

The note's twenty-row sizing arithmetic, live, on its San Jose NEM-3.0 scenario — 14,000 kWh/yr household, PG&E E-TOU-C, $3.00/Wp. The configurator column is what a tool built for legacy 1:1 net metering still recommends. The honest column recomputes as you move the two inputs every configurator buries: the shading derate from an actual roof walk and the post-NEM-3.0 export credit. Not a quote — the arithmetic you'd run before trusting one.

The reason to buy less — past the self-consumption sweet spot, the next kWp is mostly exported. At your $0.05 export credit it earns about $67/yr against $2,100 of net cost — a 31-year marginal payback. No configurator shows you this number, because no configurator is paid to show you a reason to buy less.
How the arithmetic works
Napkin-checkable, per the note: every constant is FIG.1's.
  • Yield: specific yield = 1,586 kWh/kWp/yr pre-shading (FIG.1's basis: ~1,348 ÷ 0.85) × your derate.
  • Size: the honest column holds FIG.1's sweet-spot energy target (9,706 kWh/yr ≈ 69% annual offset) — a dirtier roof needs more panels to hit the same energy, so size and cost rise as derate falls. The configurator column sizes to 100% annual offset at a 0.97 derate; it never moves, because the configurator doesn't ask.
  • Savings: 55% of generation is self-consumed (FIG.1's scenario input) and displaces imports at $0.313/kWh — the all-hours tariff-weighted rate FIG.1's savings row implies, below the $0.38 on-peak headline. The other 45% earns your export-credit slider.
  • Payback: net cost after 30% ITC ÷ annual savings. No discounting, no escalation — simple on purpose.
